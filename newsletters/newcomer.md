Newcomer's Eric Newcomer on the SpaceX IPO, contextualized as the event that will test public markets' appetite for "faith in Elon" as an asset class.

**The SpaceX IPO basics.** Expected June 2026. $75B raise target — an order of magnitude larger than what would normally be considered a massive IPO. Target valuation $1.5-2T. The combined entity is old SpaceX + old Twitter + new xAI. Financials: $18.67B revenue in 2025, lost $4.94B (xAI infrastructure spend dragged it from the $791M profit it posted in 2024 on $14B revenue).

**Revenue breakdown.** Starlink: $11.4B (60% of total), 60% EBITDA margins. Rocket launches: $4.1B. AI (xAI): $3.2B. Capex exceeded $20B last year with more than half going to AI development.

**Retail investor allocation.** Musk is allocating 30% of IPO shares to retail — roughly 3x the norm. Morgan Stanley handles smaller retail investors, Bank of America serves family offices and high-net-worth US investors, UBS takes international. Around 1,500 retail investors were invited on a roadshow tour of Starbase in Boca Chica.

**The Cursor call option.** $60B call option with $10B breakup fee. Newcomer's read: buying Cursor is partly about justifying the IPO valuation — you need something tangible to point at as proof of an AGI thesis.

**Capital concentration risk.** Google's $900M SpaceX investment from 2015 could turn into $100B+. Musk holds 40%+ of equity. Dual-class structure: Class B (insiders) = 10 votes each, Class A (public) = 1 vote. SpaceX retains "controlled company" status post-IPO, no majority-independent board required.

**The macro risk.** Bankers are pushing their own IPO clients to get out before SpaceX or wait until after summer — SpaceX's gravity is warping the IPO market. Potential Musk-believer rotation out of Tesla into SpaceX is not what Musk wants.
