### Post (hook variant 2 selected)

The word "hallucination" has always bothered me. This week, with $800M flowing into agentic AI, it started to feel like a real problem.

Replit raised $400M. Dify AI raised $30M for agentic workflows. The YC Spring 2026 batch is packed with agent companies. Capital has decided the agentic economy is real — and with it, the framing errors in how we talk about AI failures are going to start compounding.

Scott Alexander's piece this week made something click for me. AIs don't hallucinate — they guess. More specifically, they make shameless guesses, the same way a student fills in "C" on a multiple choice question they don't know. Training rewarded correct predictions and never punished incorrect ones. So the rational strategy, baked in over trillions of examples, is to always produce an answer — even when confidence is near zero. The model knows it's guessing. It just has no shame.

"Hallucination" implies something exotic and unpredictable. A machine losing its grip on reality. It lets you believe the failure mode is rare and mysterious. "Shameless guess" tells you something useful: the failure is systematic, it happens in predictable conditions — low training data, ambiguous prompts, out-of-distribution queries — and you can architect around it.

This matters enormously if you're in BD or GTM for any AI product right now. Enterprise deals get sold on "accuracy." But accuracy is an output of alignment, and alignment is genuinely unsolved. The models are doing exactly what they were trained to do.

Maybe the operators who win won't be the ones who achieve the lowest error rate. They'll be the ones who design workflows that know when to guess and when to escalate — who treat confidence calibration as a product requirement from day one. That's probably where the real value in that $800M ends up sitting.
