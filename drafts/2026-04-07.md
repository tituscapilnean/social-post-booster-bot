### Post
A startup hit $10M ARR in two months. Everyone is celebrating the speed. Almost nobody is asking what that speed does to everyone else.

Kent Beck described what's happening at the Pragmatic Summit last week as the "re-soloing" of programming. One person orchestrating ten agents, replacing a team of fifty. Martin Fowler pushed back, betting that teams get more effective rather than smaller. Both are right about different slices of the market. But both are describing a world where agents set the clock speed.

Economists are catching up to this. UChicago's Alex Imas calls task exposure "a completely meaningless tool for predicting displacement." The variable that actually matters is what happens to demand when agents collapse the cost of building to near zero. Twenty percent of new companies now charge their first customer within 30 days, up from eight percent in 2020. When agents handle the build, the bottleneck shifts from engineering capacity to knowing what's worth building.

This is where velocity gets dangerous. Agents accelerate the right hypothesis and the wrong one with equal force. The path to monopoly compresses. So does the path to shipping the wrong product faster than your competitor ships the right one. Entire org charts now operate at a tempo that used to be reserved for product sprints. The cost of iterating in the wrong direction doesn't shrink with agents. It compounds.

The agentic economy rewards the orchestrator who knows what to build. Speed without direction just compounds the wrong answer.
