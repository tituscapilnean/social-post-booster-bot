### Post

AI inference demand grew a million-fold in two years. And somehow, everyone using AI tools is working more, not less.

A new study across 163,000 employees found that after AI adoption, email volume rose 104%, chat messaging jumped 145%, and time in business tools climbed 94%. The gains didn't free anyone. They just raised the bar for what "keeping up" means.

This is the part nobody talks about. AI was supposed to compress work. Instead, it's compressing the time between actions while expanding the total volume of actions. More outputs, more messages, more decisions per hour. The bottleneck moved from production to judgment.

Boris Cherny, the creator of Claude Code, now kills 80% of what he builds before it ships. His logic is simple: when production is nearly free, the only moat is taste. The ability to look at ten drafts and know which two are worth publishing. That filter is human, and it doesn't scale the way production does.

Here's what keeps bothering me about the "AI productivity" narrative. The companies that benefit most from AI aren't the ones producing more. They're the ones with better filters, better judgment loops, tighter feedback between what gets built and what actually matters. The data backs this up: AI is consolidating markets, not fragmenting them. The top 1% of companies already capture roughly 80% of economic value, and AI is widening that gap because larger orgs have bigger datasets and can use automation to absorb the coordination costs that normally slow them down.

The real skill in the agentic economy isn't generating. Everybody can generate now. The real skill is knowing when to stop.
