### Post

Anthropic now wins 70% of new business matchups against OpenAI. Same benchmarks. Higher price. Compute-constrained. Still winning.

Ramp's March 2026 AI Index tells the story clearly. A year ago, 1 in 25 businesses on Ramp paid for Anthropic. Today it's 1 in 4. OpenAI posted its largest single-month adoption decline ever — down 1.5%. Anthropic's ARR jumped from $14B to $19B in weeks, partly on the back of a DoD standoff that Altman privately reversed while publicly supporting Amodei. U.S. ChatGPT uninstalls spiked 295% the day that story broke.

The model isn't what's winning. The system built around it is.

Every connector built, every workflow configured, every memory the system accrues creates compounding switching costs. A16z's Olivia Moore called it clearly: "context and memory compound." The Neuron put it even more plainly: no one is innovating in the general business user experience better than Anthropic right now. You're not buying a model. You're building infrastructure.

Most organizations haven't internalized this yet. Jason Lemkin at SaaStr just published 10 lessons from 10+ months running AI SDRs across four vendors. Lesson one: your human playbook has to work first. The AI is a cloning machine — feed it broken context, get broken results. GTM Strategist Maja Voje framed it this week: AI didn't change go-to-market. It exposed what was already broken. The companies that built revenue engines on cheap human labor don't need a better model. They need a different architecture — closed data loops, structured playbooks, humans overseeing agents rather than executing tasks.

That distinction is everything. Anthropic isn't winning because its model is 10% better on a benchmark. It's winning because operators building serious AI workflows have figured out that model selection is almost irrelevant compared to the quality of the system surrounding it. The model war is over. What's starting now is an infrastructure war. Your edge isn't which AI you use. It's whether you've built an operating system around it that compounds.

Most enterprise procurement conversations haven't caught up to that yet.
