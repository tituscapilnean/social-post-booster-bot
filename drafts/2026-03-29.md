### Post
The most important AI skill of 2026 has nothing to do with writing code. It has everything to do with managing people you can't see.

This week, Anthropic shipped auto mode for Claude Code, letting agents decide which actions are safe to execute without asking permission. OpenAI launched 20+ Codex plugins connecting its coding agent to Slack, Figma, and Google Drive. And on Lenny Rachitsky's podcast, a product executive described running nine specialized agents across old laptops to manage her sales, podcast, and family calendar.

The pattern is clear. We've moved past "can I build an agent?" to "can I run a team of them?" And the people doing it best are doing what good managers have always done: scoping work clearly, evaluating output honestly, building trust through iteration, and knowing when to delegate versus when to step in.

Here's the misconception most companies are stuck on. They're still hiring for prompt engineering, the ability to talk to one AI really well. The game already shifted to agent orchestration. Managing multiple specialized systems that each do one thing. Coordinating handoffs between them. Teaching each one your standards over time.

Someone put it well this week: teaching an AI your taste looks a lot like developing taste in the first place. The accumulation of many small moments, each one building like sediment on the last. That sounds more like mentoring a junior employee than writing a prompt.

The compound advantage forming right now is contextual, not technical. The operators who started building that relationship with their agents six months ago already have something no one can shortcut. And the ones starting today will have it over anyone who waits until next year.

Maybe the question worth asking isn't "should I learn to code?" anymore. It's "do I know how to manage someone I can't see?"
