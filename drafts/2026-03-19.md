### Post

Three companies, same week: Okta gave AI agents employee IDs with a kill switch. World gave shopping agents human passports. Proofpoint stopped checking identity altogether and started reading intent. They're all looking at the same problem.

The accountability model software has relied on for 30 years is structurally broken.

Every system we've built assumes actors have accounts, accounts have owners, and owners are responsible for what those accounts do. One login, one human, one chain of liability. That's false now. A single founder can deploy 50 agents. A single agent can trigger 10,000 actions. The surface area of "who did this?" just expanded by several orders of magnitude.

The misconception in circulation is that agent identity is a compliance problem — something to solve later, once agents get good enough to warrant the paperwork. The companies shipping infrastructure now see it differently. Gumloop just raised $50M to run agents at scale. Okta's new platform declares agents are "non-human employees" that need centralized access control. These aren't compliance plays. They're infrastructure bets.

Identity was boring plumbing until every company became an internet company — then Okta was worth $40B. Authorization was a checkbox until every company became a cloud company — then it was table stakes for every enterprise deal.

Every company is about to become an agent company. The authorization layer is being built right now, and most founders aren't watching it happen.
