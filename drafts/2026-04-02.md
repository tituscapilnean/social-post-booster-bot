### Post
Everyone keeps asking which jobs AI will replace. The better question: can you write handover notes for it?

This week I finished a deal-tracking system that reads my email, pulls LinkedIn messages, and updates a database with leads, contacts, deals, and account objects. It runs through Claude Code whenever I need it. My wife is building something similar for her team, an agent that drafts job descriptions and interview guides from existing examples and institutional knowledge.

These aren't toy demos. They're production workflows that used to take hours of human reasoning, pattern matching, and context switching. The common thread is that both jobs could be fully described in a handover document. Every input, every decision tree, every output format.

Trends.vc puts the agent execution gap at $52 billion. 75% of AI agent tasks still fail on basic business operations. But the failure isn't in reasoning anymore. Models can reason. The failure is in the plumbing, the integrations, the last-mile execution that connects an agent's thinking to a real system.

That gap is closing faster than most people realize. MCP hit 97 million monthly SDK downloads. Per-seat SaaS pricing is starting to crack because one agent can do the work that used to require ten people.

The misconception is that automation comes for "low-skill" work first. It doesn't. It comes for describable work. The repetitive judgment calls that thousands of people in the same company make the same way every day. If your role could be fully captured in handover notes, and there are a thousand others doing it the same way, the economics of building an agent for that role just became irresistible.

The people who stay irreplaceable aren't the ones with the most experience. They're the ones whose judgment can't be written down.
