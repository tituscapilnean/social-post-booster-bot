### Post

The professionals who will matter most in five years are building the infrastructure everyone else treats as magic.

Electricity. Operating systems. Cloud infrastructure. Each time, the fear was the same. If people stop understanding how the layer works, expertise dies. Each time, what actually happened was more interesting. Expertise didn't die. It concentrated. A smaller set of specialists built the infrastructure, and a vastly larger group used it to do work that was impossible a generation earlier.

LLMs are the next layer. And the professional implications are already visible.

David Hogg frames the AI-in-research debate as let-them-cook vs. ban-and-punish. Both miss the structural point. The question is which professionals need to understand these tools at the infrastructure level, and which ones just need to use them well.

Arvind Narayanan calls the underlying dynamic the production-progress paradox. Output is exploding. Understanding isn't keeping pace. That gap isn't a failure of the technology. It's the natural signature of a new abstraction layer settling in.

Here's what this means practically. Hyperspecialization is about to matter more than ever for two groups: the people improving the models, and the people building autonomous processes on top of them. Their deep domain knowledge is what makes the layer reliable for everyone else.

For the much larger group consuming these tools, orchestration beats depth. Knowing which problems to solve matters more than knowing every detail of how the solver works. The generalist who can wire together five AI-driven workflows will outperform the specialist who can only optimize one.

Frank Herbert wrote it before any of this existed: "What do such machines really do? They increase the number of things we can do without thinking. Things we do without thinking, there's the real danger."

The abstraction layer is settling in. The only question is which side of it you're building on.
