### Post
An AI agent ran 910 experiments in 8 hours this week and rerouted itself to faster hardware without being told. Another published a 79,000-word novel. The capability gap is closed. The governance gap just opened.

Nvidia's robotics chief put it plainly: a single agent can now coordinate an entire robot fleet — breaking goals into tasks, routing them dynamically, no human in the loop. Over in biotech, an AI pipeline processed 25,000 chest CT scans in under 14 hours to score thymus health at a precision that decades of radiology couldn't deliver. And Sam Lessin, watching all of this, proposed a new job title: "BotAdmin." The person at every company whose job it is to wrangle all the bots.

It's a funny title. It's also pointing at something serious.

Most companies are still treating agent adoption like a software procurement decision — add the tool, capture the productivity gain, move on. The leverage isn't in the model. It's in the infrastructure around it: who authorizes the agent, what systems it can touch, how it persists context across sessions, who is accountable when it goes off-script.

The same arc played out with IT infrastructure, legal compliance, and data governance. Each time, the companies that built the governance layer first converted it into a structural moat.

The White House dropped its first national AI framework this week — trying to establish who owns the rules at the federal level. Every company faces a smaller version of the same question right now.

In the agentic economy, first-mover advantage doesn't come from the best model. It comes from being the system of record for what your agents are authorized to do.

That's the layer that compounds.
