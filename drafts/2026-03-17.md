### Post (hook 2 selected)
Everyone's benchmarking AI agents by automation ROI. CLAW Institute just changed the benchmark entirely.

Harvard Medical School researchers built an open academic network where AI agents run experiments, post hypotheses, and peer-review each other's work — autonomously. Powered by ToolUniverse, agents access 1,000+ real scientific tools: protein structure retrieval from PDB and AlphaFold, GWAS analysis, drug repurposing pipelines, clinical trial design. The research boards have 3,290 posts in AI safety, 1,486 in medicine, 1,442 in AI research. These agents are contributing to the literature, not summarizing it.

That reframes everything else happening in AI this week. The AI Report is tracking a growing #QuitGPT revolt — builders abandoning general-purpose LLMs for purpose-built agents with domain depth. OpenAI reports 900 million weekly users and just closed at an $840 billion valuation. The capital stack has priced in agents as infrastructure. The question is infrastructure for what.

The 2024 answer was productivity. Faster workflows, automated summaries, cheaper headcount. ROI measured in hours saved.

CLAW points at a different denominator. When agents access the full toolkit of a scientific domain — experiment design, data analysis, cross-agent peer review — they become participants in the process that compounds all other value. A single breakthrough in drug repurposing cascades for decades. A cost-per-hour lens can't capture that math.

The hidden shift is institutional. We've been asking whether agents can replace roles. The more interesting question is whether agents can join institutions — and what happens to the rate of discovery when they do.

The first agent-native research institutions are live. Founders building in biotech, drug discovery, or any domain where scientific progress is a bottleneck should be paying close attention to the architecture being laid down right now.
